Prisoner's Dilemma Simulator

The Prisoner's Dilemma is a classic problem in game theory. Two players must independently choose whether to cooperate (C) or defect (D).

If both cooperate, they both do well. If one defects while the other cooperates, the defector gets the highest reward while the cooperator gets the worst outcome. If both defect, they both receive a poor result. In this simulation we are using the following payoff structure:

Opponent Cooperates (C) Opponent Defects (D)
You Cooperate (C) (3, 3) (0, 5)
You Defect (D) (5, 0) (1, 1)

Numbers represent (your score, opponent score). Defection is the dominant strategy in a single round because it yields a better outcome regardless of the opponent's choice.

If played once there is a clear best decision - defect. This is becasue no matter what the opponent picks, defecting will result in a better outcome than cooperating. When the game is repeated many times, different strategies can emerge — some cooperative, some exploitative. This simulator lets you explore how those strategies perform against each other in the long run. Problems of this type arise in all sorts of situations from international relations to group projects.

Tournament

The tournament allows you to pit all available strategies against each other in a round-robin format. Each pair of strategies plays the specified number of rounds, and scores are accumulated. At the end, a leaderboard shows which strategies perform best overall.

This helps you explore which strategies perform best in repeated interactions with other strategies of varied types, whether cooperative, retaliatory, or exploitative.

A little noise (1 - 5%) simulates mistakes or randomness in real life.
